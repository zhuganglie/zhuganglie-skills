# Evidence, Sourcing, and Confidence

Use this reference when answering topics with disputed chronology, modern developments, or user-requested verification.

## 1. Match Evidence to Claim Strength

- For dates, regulation, standards, technical releases, and institutional founding moments, prefer primary or official sources.
- For broad historical interpretation, use high-quality secondary sources and syntheses.
- For recent or unstable facts, verify with current sources instead of relying on memory.

## 2. Use Exact Time Markers

Prefer exact years, and use exact dates when the timing matters.

- Better: "In 1995, Netscape's IPO signaled..."
- Better when needed: "On January 9, 2007, Apple announced the iPhone..."
- Worse: "In the mid-2000s, things changed..."

## 3. Separate Fact from Interpretation

Be explicit about the difference between:

- What happened
- Why it mattered
- What pattern you infer from it

Phrase interpretive claims as analysis, not as raw fact.

## 4. Handle Boundary Choices Transparently

If the story differs sharply by geography, say which path you are analyzing.

- Global trajectory
- US-centered trajectory
- China-centered trajectory
- Europe-centered trajectory

If the user did not specify and one path dominates the evidence, say that you are treating it as the primary case.

## 5. Confidence Framing

Use a short note when needed:

- High confidence: chronology and causal interpretation are both well established.
- Medium confidence: chronology is stable, but interpretation is contested.
- Low confidence: the evidence base is thin, definitions vary, or recent events may still reshape the story.

Add at least a brief boundary note whenever you had to assume the geography or when the phase breaks, inflection points, or architect choices are interpretive rather than uniquely correct.
